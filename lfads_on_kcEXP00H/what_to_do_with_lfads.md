# What to do with LFADS — interpretation possibilities for kcEXP00H

## What LFADS fundamentally does

LFADS models your neural data as generated by a **low-dimensional dynamical system** — a recurrent neural network (GRU) whose hidden state evolves over time. The idea from the 2018 Pandarinath et al. paper is: the observed high-dimensional neural activity (spikes, or in your case ΔF/F across 75 regions) is a noisy, high-D projection of a small number of latent "factors" that govern the true population dynamics.

It's a variational autoencoder. On each trial it:
1. **Encodes** the observed data to infer a distribution over the initial network state
2. **Samples** an initial condition and lets the GRU evolve forward in time
3. **Decodes** the GRU's trajectory back to predicted data
4. Trains by maximizing the likelihood of the observed data under this model

---

## What it actually outputs (from `tuples.py` + eval notebook)

After training you get, **per trial**:

| Output | Shape | What it is |
|---|---|---|
| `factors` | `(n_trials, n_time, fac_dim)` | The low-D latent trajectory — the main output you use downstream |
| `output_params` | `(n_trials, n_time, n_regions)` | Inferred smooth rates / ΔF/F for every region on every trial |
| `ic_mean` / `ic_std` | `(n_trials, ic_dim)` | Posterior over the initial network state at t=0 |
| `co_means` / `co_stds` | `(n_trials, n_time, co_dim)` | Inferred external inputs driving the dynamics beyond the initial condition |
| `gen_states` | `(n_trials, n_time, gen_dim)` | Internal GRU hidden state |

## Concrete things you could do with your data

### 1. Single-trial denoising
With 18 trials and 75 regions, individual trial ΔF/F is noisy. `output_params` gives you a smooth, model-inferred estimate of the underlying signal **on every individual trial** — not just the trial average. This is what the evaluation notebook's first figure shows: raw → smoothed → LFADS rates, where LFADS rates are much cleaner and respect the dynamical structure of the population.

### 2. Population trajectory analysis in a shared low-D space
`factors` gives you the brain state as a point in ~10–20 dimensional space at each timepoint. You can ask: do odour-only, walking-only, visual-only, and combined-stimulus trials trace out distinguishable trajectories? This is richer than per-region AUC/slope metrics because it captures the **joint** evolution of all 75 regions simultaneously. The evaluation notebook does exactly this — 3D PC plots of factor trajectories colored by condition.

### 3. Testing the summation hypothesis in latent space
The current `kcAN00H_005-B` analysis tests whether `ΔF/F(xOF) ≈ ΔF/F(xOx) + ΔF/F(xxF)` at the signal level. With LFADS you could ask the same question in **latent space**: does `factors(xOF) ≈ factors(xOx) + factors(xxF)`? If yes, linear summation holds at the dynamical level. If no — even when it holds in the raw signal — that would be a deeper finding about how the brain integrates stimuli.

### 4. Decoding stimulus identity or behaviour
The evaluation notebook trains a linear ridge regression from `factors` to joystick velocity (R² ≈ 0.85). For your data you could decode: condition identity, odour presence, forward-walking state, or any behavioural variable you have. Because factors are low-D and denoised, they typically decode better than raw ΔF/F.

### 5. Initial condition analysis
`ic_mean` captures the brain state at the very start of each trial. You could ask: does the pre-stimulus state (before odour onset) predict anything about the response — e.g., does high arousal/locomotion state at trial start predict a stronger odour response? This is a question about **trial-by-trial variability** that condition-mean analyses cannot address.

### 6. Cross-fly generalization (the multisession use case)
The evaluation notebook's key result: train a decoder on 11 sessions, test on the 12th — does the decoder still work? For your 5 flies this would ask: **do flies share the same latent dynamics?** If factors trained on 4 flies decode condition identity in the 5th fly without any retraining, you've shown the population dynamics are conserved across individuals. If not, the per-fly readin matrices are absorbing genuine fly-to-fly differences.

### 7. Controller outputs as inferred stimulus drive
If you enable the controller (`co_dim > 0`), `co_means` captures time-varying external inputs inferred by the model — stuff that's driving the dynamics but isn't explained by the initial condition alone. In your case, the odour, walking, and visual stimuli are all external drives. You could examine whether `co_means` tracks stimulus onset times and separates by modality.

## What LFADS is NOT good for

- **Small trial counts**: With 18 trials per fly, the variational inference may be poorly constrained. The model works best with hundreds of trials. This is the main practical concern for kcEXP00H.
- **Region-level interpretability**: Factors are linear combinations of all 75 regions. If you want to know "what does AME-R specifically do," region-level analyses are more direct.
- **Causality / connectivity**: LFADS infers dynamics but makes no claims about what drives what between regions.

## Reconstruction type for ΔF/F data

The tutorial uses `Poisson` (for spike counts). For ΔF/F use `Gaussian` or `MSE` — both are already implemented in `lfads_torch/modules/recons.py`. The config just needs:

```yaml
reconstruction:
  - _target_: lfads_torch.modules.recons.Gaussian
```
